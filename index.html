Baughman Township is the only township in all of Wayne County with ZERO dedicated fire levy funding. Every neighboring township has been paying for fire protection for years.

Concern #1 — The Most Common Reason People Voted No Legal Protections & Trust "I don't trust the township with my money." — Here is why that concern doesn't apply to a levy fund.
Could levy money be used for lawsuits like before?
No — and this is the most important fact on this page. The lawsuits you've heard about were paid from the township's general fund — the regular checking account that trustees control. A fire/EMS levy creates a completely different account, restricted by Ohio state law. Trustees have zero legal authority to move levy money anywhere else. It works exactly the same way the road & bridge fund works — that money can only fix roads, and the state auditor verifies it every year. The levy fund works identically.
💡 The Two Jars Explanation

Think of the township as having two separate money jars on a shelf. The first jar (General Fund) is a regular jar trustees can spend on many things — including lawyers when disputes arise. That's the jar that paid for past lawsuits. The second jar (Levy Fund) has a padlock on it that only the State of Ohio holds the key to. The label on that jar says "Fire and EMS Contracts Only." No trustee, no dispute, no lawsuit can open that jar for anything else. These are not the same jar.

🔒 What Ohio Law Says Levy Money Can and Cannot Do

Levy Money CAN Pay For:

Fire department contracts (Orrville, Marshallville, North Lawrence)
EMS / paramedic service contracts
Fire and rescue equipment
Emergency response operations

Levy Money CANNOT Pay For:

Lawsuits or attorney fees — ever
Road repairs or any other township service
Trustee salaries or bonuses
Any non-fire/EMS expense whatsoever
Ohio Revised Code § 5705 — The Ohio State Auditor reviews township accounts annually. Misuse means personal repayment liability and potential criminal charges for any official who violates this. It's the law, not a promise.
⚖️
Ohio Revised Code § 5705 — Statutory Authority

Ohio law requires that all levy proceeds be deposited into a separate, dedicated fund that can only be spent for the purpose stated on the ballot. Township trustees are personally liable — including potential criminal charges — for any misuse of restricted fund money. The Ohio State Auditor performs mandatory annual reviews of all township accounts to verify compliance. This protection exists by statute, not by promise or trust.

What were those lawsuits actually about?
In 2018, Baughman Township left the East Wayne Joint Fire District — a shared regional fire arrangement. When they left, there was a dispute over how the shared savings account should be divided. The township believed it was owed far more than the County Auditor calculated ($50,000–$200,000 vs. the auditor's figure of about $2,800). Courts ultimately dismissed the main case in December 2021. Those legal battles are over. This levy has nothing to do with that dispute — it creates new, restricted money that goes directly to the three fire departments serving Baughman residents today.
Timeline of Legal Events
  • 2018 Baughman Township exits the East Wayne Joint Fire District. A shared regional fire arrangement ends, triggering a dispute over division of the shared savings account.
  • 2018 – 2021 Township pursues legal action, believing it was owed $50,000–$200,000 from shared funds. County Auditor calculated the figure at approximately $2,800. Dispute is litigated in courts using the general fund — not any levy.
  • December 2021 Courts dismiss the main case. The legal dispute over the East Wayne Joint Fire District exit is over. No ongoing litigation. No pending cases related to this matter.
  • Today The township contracts with three fire departments for coverage. The proposed levy creates a new, legally restricted fund that did not exist during any of those legal disputes and is structurally immune to the same misuse.
Does voting No send a message to the trustees?
Voting No at a levy election does not remove a trustee, affect their pay, or change their position. Trustees are held accountable at trustee elections — those are the elections to watch if you want change in leadership. Voting No on the levy only removes fire and EMS funding from the township. The trustees stay in place either way. Your fire protection and your trustee accountability are two completely separate decisions at two different elections.

📞 WHO WOULD YOU CALL?

If the levy fails and contracts lapse, Wayne County 911 would still answer your call — but dispatchers would have no contracted department obligated to respond to Baughman Township. Response would depend entirely on whatever mutual aid a neighboring department chose to provide that day, at their discretion, after their own township's calls are handled first. That is not a system. That is hope.

Is the township required by law to have fire or EMS coverage?
No. Ohio Attorney General Opinion No. 94-067 is plain: townships are not legally required to provide fire or EMS services. That means when the money runs out, there is no law that forces anyone to show up. No backup. No fallback. The township would simply have no fire or EMS coverage until something new is arranged — which takes time the residents of Baughman Township may not have in an emergency.
⚖️
Ohio AG Opinion No. 94-067 — What It Means for Your Family

The Ohio Attorney General formally concluded that township trustees have no legal duty to establish or maintain fire or EMS service. Fire protection is a community choice — and that choice requires funding. Without this levy, the general fund — which has run a deficit every year since 2020 — is the only thing standing between Baughman Township and zero fire coverage.

Orrville already said no — what does that mean for us?
Orrville Fire Department has formally told the township it will not provide service without a funded contract. Marshallville and North Lawrence have taken the same position. These are not threats — they are the ordinary business of running a fire department. They have paid staff, equipment, and their own communities to serve first. Without a contract, they have no obligation to cross into Baughman Township. When the levy fails, the contracts expire, and there is no plan B.

What about mutual aid — won't neighboring departments still help?
Mutual aid exists for overflow situations — when a department's own township needs them at the same time as a neighbor. It is not a substitute for a contract. Mutual aid is voluntary, discretionary, and always secondary to a department's own paying customers. Relying on mutual aid as Baughman Township's primary fire coverage would be like relying on a neighbor's truck because you sold your own car. It might work sometimes. It will not work when it matters most.
Our home insurance — would it be affected?
Yes, very likely. Insurance companies use ISO fire protection ratings to set premiums. Baughman Township's current rating is based on having contracted fire coverage nearby. If contracts lapse and there is no organized fire response, the ISO rating would likely drop — and homeowner insurance premiums would increase to reflect the higher risk. The levy's cost per year may well be less than the insurance premium increase from losing coverage.
If we vote yes now, are we stuck forever?
No. This levy is temporary — five years only. It expires in 2031 automatically. Voters decide again at that point, or it simply ends. The five-year window also gives the township time to explore long-term structural options: a joint fire district with a neighboring township, a new regional service model, or other arrangements. A YES vote in May is not a permanent commitment. It is five years of breathing room to keep your neighbors covered while the community works toward a lasting solution.
